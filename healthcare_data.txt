Topic: Common cold | Care focuses on rest, fluids, and comfort measures rather than antibiotics. Most colds resolve within 7–10 days. Wash hands frequently to reduce spread. Over-the-counter decongestants or antihistamines may relieve symptoms temporarily.

Topic: Influenza | Early antiviral treatment (such as oseltamivir) may help high-risk patients when started promptly within 48 hours of symptom onset. High-risk groups include elderly, young children, pregnant women, and those with chronic conditions. Annual vaccination is the best prevention.

Topic: COVID-19 | Follow current local guidance for testing, isolation, and treatment. Stay up to date with recommended vaccines and boosters. Symptoms range from mild to severe. High-risk individuals should seek prompt medical evaluation. Long COVID can cause lingering symptoms in some people.

Topic: Sinusitis | Most sinusitis cases are viral and resolve without antibiotics. Persistent symptoms lasting more than 10 days, or worsening after initial improvement, may suggest bacterial sinusitis requiring medical evaluation. Saline rinses and steam inhalation can provide relief.

Topic: Asthma | Controller inhalers (corticosteroids) are used daily for long-term management, while rescue inhalers (short-acting bronchodilators) address acute symptoms. Identifying and avoiding triggers—such as allergens, exercise, cold air, or smoke—is key to management.

Topic: COPD | COPD is primarily caused by smoking. Quitting smoking is the single most important step to slow progression. Treatment includes bronchodilators, pulmonary rehabilitation, and supplemental oxygen in advanced cases. Annual flu and pneumonia vaccines are recommended.

Topic: Sore throat | Seek care if there is high fever, difficulty swallowing, severe throat pain, or symptoms lasting longer than a week — these may suggest strep throat or other bacterial infections requiring antibiotics. Gargling warm salt water may provide temporary relief.

Topic: Cough | Staying hydrated and using humidified air can help. A cough lasting over three weeks should be evaluated by a clinician to rule out causes such as asthma, GERD, or less commonly, a more serious condition.

Topic: Allergies | Reducing exposure to allergens (pollen, dust, pet dander) and using antihistamines or nasal corticosteroid sprays can help relieve symptoms. Allergy testing can identify specific triggers. Immunotherapy (allergy shots) can reduce sensitivity over time.

Topic: Vaccines | Other recommended vaccines include shingles (Shingrix) for adults 50+, pneumococcal vaccine, hepatitis B, and HPV vaccine for younger adults. Keep an immunization record. Talk to a clinician about which vaccines are right for you based on age, health, and lifestyle.

Topic: Hand hygiene | Wash hands before eating, after using the restroom, after touching high-contact surfaces, and after coughing or sneezing. When soap and water are unavailable, hand sanitizer with at least 60% alcohol is effective against most germs.

Topic: Blood pressure | Elevated blood pressure (hypertension) increases the risk of heart attack and stroke. Lifestyle changes—exercise, reduced sodium, weight management, limiting alcohol, and stress reduction—are first-line interventions. Medications are often needed for sustained hypertension.

Topic: Hypertension risks | Regular blood pressure screening is important because hypertension often has no symptoms — it's the 'silent killer.' Starting in early adulthood, annual screenings help catch elevated readings early when lifestyle changes are most effective.

Topic: Cholesterol | Diet (limiting saturated and trans fats), regular aerobic exercise, and maintaining healthy weight can improve cholesterol levels. Statins and other medications are prescribed when lifestyle changes aren't sufficient. HDL ('good') cholesterol is protective.

Topic: Heart disease | Prevention focuses on lifestyle modification and regular medical care. Smoking cessation dramatically reduces risk. A heart-healthy diet, regular exercise, and managing blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar are the cornerstones of prevention.

Topic: Stroke signs | Every minute counts during a stroke. Additional signs include sudden severe headache, vision changes, and confusion. Call emergency services immediately — clot-busting medication must be given within a narrow time window to be effective.

Topic: Chest pain | While not all chest pain is cardiac, it should be taken seriously. Other causes include acid reflux, muscle strain, anxiety, or lung issues. Do not wait to see if it passes — call emergency services for unexplained chest pain, especially with other symptoms.

Topic: Diabetes overview | Common symptoms include increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow wound healing. Type 1 requires insulin; Type 2 is often managed with lifestyle changes and oral medications. Regular HbA1c testing monitors long-term control.

Topic: Diabetes diet | Consistent meal timing helps maintain stable blood glucose levels. Focus on non-starchy vegetables, lean proteins, whole grains, and healthy fats. Work with a registered dietitian for personalized guidance. Portion control and limiting sugary drinks are essential.

Topic: Hypoglycemia | The 15-15 rule: consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate (4 glucose tablets, 4 oz juice), wait 15 minutes, recheck. If still low, repeat. After recovery, eat a small snack. Severe hypoglycemia may require glucagon emergency kit.

Topic: Hyperglycemia | Follow your diabetes care plan — don't skip medications or insulin. Sick days can raise blood sugar significantly. Seek urgent help if blood sugar is very high, you have nausea/vomiting, or cannot keep fluids down.

Topic: Weight management | Even modest weight loss (5-10% of body weight) can significantly improve blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol in people at risk. Focus on gradual, sustainable changes — about 0.5 to 1 kg per week is reasonable.

Topic: BMI | Clinicians consider BMI alongside waist circumference, body composition, metabolic markers, and overall health. A BMI of 18.5–24.9 is generally considered healthy, but fitness, muscle mass, and ethnicity affect interpretation.

Topic: Nutrition basics | Limit added sugars and highly processed foods. Fill half your plate with vegetables and fruits at each meal. Choose whole grains over refined. Include legumes, nuts, seeds, and fish regularly for diverse nutrients.

Topic: Fiber intake | Adults need 25–38 grams of fiber daily, yet most get far less. Sources include whole grains, legumes, fruits, vegetables, nuts, and seeds. Increase fiber gradually and drink plenty of water to avoid digestive discomfort.

Topic: Sodium reduction | Most sodium in the diet comes from processed, packaged, and restaurant foods — not the salt shaker. Cooking at home, reading labels, using herbs and spices for flavor, and choosing low-sodium products are effective strategies.

Topic: Healthy fats | Limit saturated fats (found in red meat, full-fat dairy, tropical oils) and avoid trans fats. Replace with monounsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado) and polyunsaturated fats (fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed).

Topic: Hydration | Thirst and urine color (pale yellow = well hydrated; dark yellow/amber = drink more) are practical guides. Water is the best choice. Caffeinated and sugary beverages are less optimal. Most adults need about 2–3 liters of fluid daily.

Topic: Exercise guidelines | This breaks down to about 30 minutes, 5 days a week. Alternatively, 75 minutes of vigorous activity. Include muscle-strengthening activities at least 2 days per week. Even small amounts of movement are beneficial — start where you are.

Topic: Strength training | Include all major muscle groups: legs, back, chest, shoulders, arms, core. Allow 48 hours of recovery between sessions for the same muscle group. Progressive overload — gradually increasing resistance or reps — is key to continued improvement.

Topic: Flexibility | Especially beneficial after long periods of sitting. Hold static stretches for 20–30 seconds. Dynamic stretching is better as a warm-up before exercise. Yoga and pilates can improve both flexibility and core strength.

Topic: Sleep hygiene | Adults need 7–9 hours of sleep. Keep your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet. Avoid screens for at least an hour before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin. Avoid large meals and alcohol close to bedtime.

Topic: Insomnia | Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the most effective long-term treatment — more effective than sleep medications. Sleep restriction therapy, stimulus control, and relaxation techniques are key components.

Topic: Stress management | Regular physical activity, mindfulness meditation, deep breathing exercises, and maintaining social connections all help buffer stress. Identify your stress triggers and develop coping strategies. Consider professional support for chronic stress.

Topic: Anxiety | Anxiety disorders are among the most common mental health conditions. Effective treatments include cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), exposure therapy, and medication. Lifestyle factors like sleep, exercise, and caffeine reduction also play a role.

Topic: Depression | Depression is treatable. Seek professional help if symptoms persist for more than two weeks or interfere with daily functioning. Treatment includes psychotherapy, medication, or a combination. Exercise has significant antidepressant effects.

Topic: Mental health support | Mental health is as important as physical health. If you are struggling, talking to a trusted friend, family member, or professional is a sign of strength. Many resources are available — including telehealth options.

Topic: Gastroenteritis | Focus on fluid replacement to prevent dehydration. Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are more effective than plain water for replacing lost electrolytes. Seek care if dehydration is severe, blood is present in stool, or symptoms persist beyond a week.

Topic: GERD | Avoiding late meals, lying down after eating, trigger foods (fatty meals, caffeine, alcohol, spicy food), and tight clothing can help. Elevating the head of the bed helps some people. Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are common medications.

Topic: Constipation | Increase fiber intake gradually, drink more fluids, and increase physical activity. Osmotic laxatives (like polyethylene glycol) are safe for short-term use. Persistent constipation, especially with new onset, should be medically evaluated.

Topic: Diarrhea | Most acute diarrhea is caused by viral infections or food contamination and resolves within 2–3 days. Stay hydrated with water or ORS. Avoid dairy and high-fiber foods temporarily. BRAT diet (bananas, rice, applesauce, toast) can ease symptoms.

Topic: Food safety | The 'danger zone' for bacterial growth is 4–60°C. Refrigerate leftovers within 2 hours. Cook poultry to 74°C, beef to 71°C. Wash produce and keep raw meats separate from other foods. When in doubt, throw it out.

Topic: First aid cuts | Rinse minor wounds with clean water and mild soap. Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth for 10-15 minutes for bleeding. See a doctor if the wound is deep, won't stop bleeding, has jagged edges, was caused by a dirty or rusty object, or if tetanus vaccination is not current.

Topic: First aid burns | Cover loosely with a non-stick sterile dressing. Do not use ice, butter, toothpaste, or other home remedies — these can worsen the burn. Seek medical care for burns larger than 3cm, burns on face/hands/genitals/joints, or deep burns.

Topic: First aid fractures | Do not try to realign the bone. Splint above and below the fracture site using padding and rigid material. Apply ice wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling. Seek emergency care for open fractures (bone breaks skin), suspected spinal injuries, or if circulation is impaired.

Topic: First aid sprains | Rest the injured area. Apply ice for 20 minutes at a time (wrapped in cloth — never directly on skin). Use a compression bandage. Elevate above heart level. See a clinician if the area looks deformed, can't bear weight, or doesn't improve in 2–3 days.

Topic: Choking | For adults: perform the Heimlich maneuver (abdominal thrusts). For infants: use back blows and chest thrusts. For yourself alone: use a chair or countertop edge. First aid training through certified courses is strongly recommended.

Topic: Head injury | Even seemingly minor head injuries can cause serious complications. Watch for deteriorating consciousness, repeated vomiting, one pupil larger than the other, seizures, or weakness in limbs. Never leave someone with a suspected serious head injury alone.

Topic: Emergency warning signs | Other emergencies include severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), overdose, seizures, sudden vision loss, and signs of shock (pale/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion). When in doubt, call emergency services. It is always better to err on the side of caution.

Topic: When to see a doctor | Don't ignore symptoms that linger beyond expected recovery time, worsen despite home treatment, or significantly affect daily functioning. Telehealth options make access easier. Trust your instincts — you know your body.

Topic: Fever | Keeping hydrated and using acetaminophen or ibuprofen may reduce fever-related discomfort. Seek care for very high fever (above 39.4°C), fever with severe headache or stiff neck, fever lasting more than 3 days, or fever in infants under 3 months.

Topic: Dehydration | Treatment is oral fluids or oral rehydration solutions for mild to moderate cases. Severe dehydration requires medical attention and may need intravenous fluids. At-risk groups include the elderly, infants, and those with vomiting or diarrhea.

Topic: Pain relievers | Acetaminophen (paracetamol) is safer for those with stomach issues, kidney problems, or heart disease. Ibuprofen is an NSAID that reduces inflammation — avoid with kidney disease, peptic ulcers, or in late pregnancy. Always follow dosing instructions and maximum daily limits.

Topic: Medication safety | Tell every healthcare provider about all medications, supplements, and herbal products you take. Avoid mixing medicines without guidance — interactions can be serious. Use one pharmacy when possible. Check expiration dates and store medications properly.

Topic: Antibiotics | Inappropriate antibiotic use leads to antibiotic resistance, making infections harder to treat. It also causes side effects including diarrhea and increased infection risk. Always complete the full course when prescribed, and never use leftover antibiotics.

Topic: Smoking cessation | The benefits begin within hours of quitting and continue for years. Effective methods include nicotine replacement therapy (patches, gum, lozenges), prescription medications (varenicline, bupropion), and behavioral counseling. Combining pharmacotherapy with counseling gives the best results.

Topic: Alcohol limits | Current guidelines recommend no more than 2 standard drinks per day and no more than 14 per week for men; for women, no more than 1 per day and 7 per week. Many guidelines now suggest that any amount carries some health risk.

Topic: Skin health | Apply broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen even on cloudy days. Use the ABCDE rule for moles: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color changes, Diameter over 6mm, Evolution over time. See a dermatologist for any concerning skin changes.

Topic: Kidney health | Other protective measures include staying hydrated, avoiding excessive NSAID use, not smoking, and limiting alcohol. Get regular kidney function tests (creatinine, GFR) if you have diabetes, hypertension, or family history of kidney disease.

Topic: Liver health | Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is increasingly common, linked to obesity, diabetes, and metabolic syndrome. Hepatitis B and C vaccines and screenings are also important. Avoid sharing needles or personal hygiene items to prevent hepatitis transmission.

Topic: Eye health | Adults should have comprehensive eye exams every 1–2 years, or as recommended by their eye doctor. UV-blocking sunglasses protect against cataracts and macular degeneration. Screen time breaks (20-20-20 rule: every 20 min, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) reduce eye strain.

Topic: Dental health | See a dentist every 6–12 months for cleanings and checkups. Gum (periodontal) disease is linked to heart disease, diabetes complications, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. Limit sugary and acidic foods and drinks.

Topic: Ear care | The ear canal is self-cleaning — earwax normally moves out on its own. Cotton swabs push wax deeper and can damage the eardrum. Protect hearing with earplugs in loud environments. Manage excess earwax with over-the-counter drops or professional removal.

Topic: Pregnancy basics | Start folic acid (400-800mcg daily) before conception if possible to reduce neural tube defects. Attend all prenatal appointments. Avoid raw fish, unpasteurized dairy, and deli meats. Vaccinations (flu, Tdap) are recommended during pregnancy.

Topic: Child nutrition | Introduce a wide variety of foods early to reduce pickiness. Avoid using food as a reward or punishment. Limit fruit juice — whole fruit is better. Model healthy eating habits. Iron, calcium, and vitamin D are important nutrients for growing children.
